Course Description

This course provides a comprehensive introduction to monetary economics, financial institutions, and monetary policy. We explore how the Federal Reserve system functions, how interest rates are determined, how banks create money, and how monetary policy affects the broader economy. Through lectures, case studies, interactive worksheets, hands-on experiments, and a capstone project, students develop a deep understanding of the financial system and monetary policy tools.

Money and Banking is the study of decision-making at the financial system level. How do central banks manage the money supply? What determines interest rates? How do monetary policy decisions affect inflation, employment, and economic growth? This course explores these critical questions through core economic principles applied to the financial sector.

Course Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course, you will be able to:

  1. Understand financial system structure - Grasp the organization of the Federal Reserve System, types of financial institutions, and their roles
  2. Analyze interest rate determination - Apply economic theory to explain how interest rates are determined in different markets
  3. Explain money creation - Understand how banks create money through the lending process and money multiplier mechanics
  4. Evaluate monetary policy tools - Compare and critique different monetary policy instruments (open market operations, discount rate, reserve requirements)
  5. Assess policy transmission - Analyze how monetary policy decisions work through the economy to affect inflation, unemployment, and growth
  6. Apply real-world analysis - Connect course concepts to current financial events, policy debates, and banking crises
  7. Think like a monetary economist - Recognize how financial incentives, regulations, and expectations shape money and banking

2. Monetary Policy Project (200 pts)

Worth: 200 points (25% of grade)
Team Size: 3-4 students
Duration: 10 weeks (Feb 25 - May 8)

Project Mission

Analyze a major Federal Reserve monetary policy decision and evaluate its effectiveness. Your analysis should apply monetary economic principles to assess how and why the Fed made specific policy choices and what their economic impacts were.

Project Philosophy

We‚Äôre not looking for purely academic exercises. We‚Äôre looking for economic thinking applied to real monetary policy decisions. The best projects show:

  • Clear understanding of the economic context when the decision was made
  • Rigorous application of monetary theory to explain Fed actions
  • Honest assessment of policy effectiveness using real economic data
  • Thoughtful evaluation of alternative policy approaches the Fed might have considered

Project Phases

Phase 1: Policy Decision Identification (Due March 4)

Deliverable: 1-2 page proposal

Requirements: - Identify a significant Fed monetary policy decision (2008 financial crisis, 2020 pandemic response, recent rate changes, etc.) - Explain the economic context and why the decision was important - Describe what the Fed did (specific policy tools, magnitudes, timing) - Identify your team members and their roles - Provide initial bibliography (3+ sources)

Examples of Major Decisions: - 2008-2009: Emergency lending facilities and quantitative easing - 2011-2014: Extended duration of low rates (‚Äúforward guidance‚Äù) - 2015-2018: Interest rate normalization - 2020: Pandemic emergency response - 2021-2023: Inflation and rate hiking cycle

Phase 2: Economic Context & Theory (Due April 8)

Deliverable: 3-5 page progress report + supporting data

Requirements: - Economic Situation: What was happening in the economy? (inflation, unemployment, growth, financial stress) - Monetary Theory: Which economic theories explain the Fed‚Äôs decision? (Classical, Keynesian, Monetarist, etc.) - Policy Transmission: How do these policy tools work through the economy? - Alternative Theories: How would different theoretical perspectives have recommended different policies? - Initial Assessment: What do you predict will happen?

Data to Gather: - Fed press releases and FOMC statements - Economic indicators (inflation, employment, GDP growth) - Financial market data (interest rates, stock markets, credit spreads) - Academic papers analyzing the policy decision - Historical data before, during, and after the policy change

Phase 3: Effectiveness Analysis & Evaluation (Due April 29)

Deliverable: 10-15 page report with economic analysis + Excel spreadsheet

Requirements:

A. Timeline & Events - What exactly did the Fed do? (Specific policy tools, dates, magnitudes) - How was policy communicated? (Press releases, speeches, forward guidance) - How did markets react? (Interest rates, credit spreads, asset prices)

B. Monetary Transmission Analysis - Interest rate channel: How did Fed policy affect interest rates throughout the economy? - Credit channel: How did policy affect bank lending and credit availability? - Asset price channel: How did policy affect stock prices, house prices, and other assets? - Exchange rate channel: How did policy affect the dollar and international trade? - Expectations channel: How did forward guidance shape economic expectations?

C. Outcome Evaluation - Did inflation respond as predicted? - Did unemployment/employment respond as predicted? - Did growth respond as predicted? - Quantify the impacts using data (before/after comparisons, regressions if appropriate)

D. Effectiveness Assessment - Did the policy achieve its stated goals? - Were there unintended consequences? - Could alternative policies have worked better? - What did we learn from this policy experience?

E. Policy Comparison - How does this policy compare to historical precedent? - What would other economists have recommended? - Was the policy better or worse than the alternatives?

Supporting Excel Spreadsheet: - Key economic data series over time - Graphs of policy effects (interest rates, inflation, employment, etc.) - Timeline of policy actions and market reactions - Summary statistics before/after policy change

Phase 4: Final Presentation (May 4-8)

Deliverable: 12-minute live presentation + final written submission

Presentation Components:

  1. The Economic Problem (2.5 min)
    • Economic conditions when Fed made decision
    • Why action was needed
    • Data showing severity of situation
  2. The Policy Decision (2.5 min)
    • What the Fed decided to do
    • Policy tools used
    • Why this approach makes economic sense
  3. The Transmission (3 min)
    • How policy worked through the economy
    • Timeline of effects
    • Data showing policy impacts
  4. The Verdict (2.5 min)
    • Did the policy work?
    • Success in achieving goals
    • Trade-offs or unintended consequences
    • Overall assessment
  5. Q&A (1.5 min)
    • Be ready to defend your analysis
    • Discuss alternative policies

Presentation Format: - 3‚Äô √ó 4‚Äô poster board (or equivalent digital poster) - Professional one-page policy brief - Excel model/graphs visible - Clear timeline of events - Color, visuals, data-driven

3. Experiments (2 √ó 50 pts = 100 pts)

Purpose: Conduct economic experiments that demonstrate how financial markets and monetary policy work in practice.

Experiment 1: Interest Rate Determination (Due February 19 - 50 pts)

Objective: Understand how supply and demand in loanable funds markets determines interest rates

What You‚Äôll Do: - Participate in a loanable funds market simulation - Act as borrower or lender with assigned demand/supply schedules - Trade loans at negotiated interest rates - Track how interest rates change as market equilibrates

Your Deliverable: 3-page report including:

  1. Market Data (10 pts)
    • Record of all transactions (who, when, quantity, rate)
    • Calculation of equilibrium interest rate
    • Comparison: actual rate vs. theoretical prediction
  2. Supply & Demand Analysis (20 pts)
    • Graph showing actual supply and demand curves
    • Identify equilibrium point
    • Calculate consumer and producer surplus
    • Explain how surplus changed as market operated
  3. Economic Interpretation (15 pts)
    • How did interest rates change? Why?
    • Did market reach equilibrium? How do you know?
    • What frictions prevented equilibrium?
    • How does this reflect real financial markets?
  4. Personal Reflection (5 pts)
    • How did incentives affect YOUR borrowing/lending decisions?
    • Would you have made different choices with different circumstances?

Experiment 2: Monetary Policy Effects (Due April 1 - 50 pts)

Objective: Explore how monetary policy decisions ripple through the economy

What You‚Äôll Do: - Participate in a macroeconomic simulation involving money, lending, and real activity - Represent households, firms, banks, and the central bank - Make decisions about saving, borrowing, investment, and lending - Observe how monetary policy affects inflation, employment, and growth

Your Deliverable: 3-page report including:

  1. Simulation Data (10 pts)
    • Document key decisions made during simulation
    • Record outcomes (inflation, unemployment, growth, interest rates)
    • Compare actual outcomes to theoretical predictions
  2. Monetary Transmission Analysis (20 pts)
    • Show how money supply changes affected other variables
    • Graph relationships between money, interest rates, and real activity
    • Calculate money multiplier and velocity effects
  3. Policy Effectiveness (15 pts)
    • Did monetary policy work as predicted by theory?
    • Which transmission mechanisms were strongest?
    • What frictions prevented policy from working perfectly?
    • How realistic is the simulation model?
  4. Reflection on Real Policy (5 pts)
    • How does this simulation compare to real monetary policy?
    • What does it suggest about effectiveness of Fed actions?
    • What mechanisms might work differently in reality?
