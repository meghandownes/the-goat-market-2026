Grading & Assessment Structure

Overview

Your grade in ECON2110G is based on four types of assessments totaling 800 points. This grading system emphasizes consistent engagement, deep understanding, practical application, and collaborative policy development.

Assessment Components

1. Class Worksheets (400 points total)

Purpose: Reinforce key concepts through problem-solving and real-world application

Format: - 20 worksheets throughout the semester - One per class session (mostly) - Named format: MMDD-topic-ws.qmd - Example: 0127-scarcity-ws.qmd, 0129-opportunity-cost-ws.qmd

Scoring: - Each worksheet: 20 points - 20 worksheets √ó 20 points = 400 points - Graded on completion, effort, and correctness - Worksheets are completed during class time

Topics Covered: - Scarcity and economic thinking - Opportunity cost and marginal analysis - Quantitative methods and unit analysis - Economic measurement (GDP, inflation, growth) - Growth models (Solow, Keynesian, Monetary, AD/AS) - Policy analysis and applications

Late Work Policy: - Submitted same day: Full credit (20 points) - Submitted within 1 week: Half credit (10 points) - After 1 week: No credit (0 points) - Note: Worksheets missed due to absence can be made up, but late penalties apply

2. In-Class Experiments (100 points total)

Purpose: Develop intuition for economic principles through experiential learning

Format: - 2 experiments throughout the semester - Experiment 1 (Week 3): Marginal thinking and decision-making - Experiment 2 (Week 6): Growth, compound effects, and exponential processes

Scoring: - Each experiment: 50 points - 2 experiments √ó 50 points = 100 points - Graded on participation, data collection, and reflection

What to Expect: - Market simulations and trading exercises - Individual decision-making tasks - Group problem-solving challenges - Real-time data collection and analysis - Post-experiment reflective writing

Participation Requirements: - Attendance is mandatory - Active participation in all phases (experiment, data collection, analysis) - Completion of experiment documentation - Reflection on findings and economic implications

3. Economics Notebook (100 points total)

Purpose: Build a reflective practice connecting theory to lived experience

Format: - Semester-long reflective journal - 3 major reflection entries (roughly every 5 weeks) - Each entry: 500-750 words - Submitted electronically in Quarto markdown format

Scoring: - Entry 1 (Due Week 5, Feb 19): 33 points - Entry 2 (Due Week 10, Apr 2): 33 points - Entry 3 (Due Week 14, Apr 30): 34 points - Total: 100 points

Reflection Prompts (Examples): - How has studying scarcity and opportunity cost changed how you think about your own decisions? - What surprised you most about economic growth models, and why? - How do different economic theories help explain a current event or policy debate? - Which economic principle has the most relevance to your future career? - What misconceptions about the economy did you have before this course?

Grading Criteria: - Depth of Reflection (40%): Goes beyond surface level; shows genuine engagement - Connection to Course Content (30%): Explicitly links personal observations to concepts from class - Clarity & Writing (20%): Clear, organized, well-written entries - Evidence of Growth (10%): Shows evolving understanding over the semester

Format Requirements: - Use Quarto markdown (.qmd format) - Include citations where referencing specific course materials - Each entry should be 500-750 words - Include the entry number and date in the header

4. Semester Project: Let‚Äôs Play Policy: Who‚Äôs The GOAT? (200 points total)

Purpose: Synthesize semester learning to develop and present original policy recommendations

Overview: This capstone project asks you to take an economic policy question and develop evidence-based recommendations. You‚Äôll work in groups of 2-3 to create a policy brief and presentation, answering: ‚ÄúWhat is the greatest economic policy approach of all time (GOAT) for addressing this issue?‚Äù

Project Components:

A. Policy Brief (100 points)

A 2,000-2,500 word written analysis including:

  1. Issue Definition (20 points)
    • Clear articulation of the macroeconomic problem
    • Why it matters (economic and social significance)
    • Current state of the problem with data
  2. Theoretical Analysis (30 points)
    • Which economic theories best explain the problem?
    • How do different theories suggest different causes?
    • What does each theory predict about solutions?
  3. Policy Options (30 points)
    • At least 3 different policy approaches examined
    • Pros and cons of each approach
    • Evidence from case studies or research
  4. Recommendation & Justification (20 points)
    • Your recommended policy approach
    • Why this policy is the ‚ÄúGOAT‚Äù (Greatest Approach of All Time)
    • Implementation considerations

B. Presentation (75 points)

A 15-20 minute group presentation including:

  • Content Clarity (25 points): Clear explanation of issue, analysis, and recommendation
  • Visual Design (20 points): Well-organized slides with appropriate visuals and data
  • Delivery (20 points): Confident presentation, good time management, engagement with audience
  • Q&A (10 points): Thoughtful responses to audience questions

C. Peer Evaluation (25 points)

  • Each student evaluates peer contributions
  • Ensures accountability and fair distribution of work
  • Peer feedback affects individual scores

Timeline: - Week 12-13: Topic selection and group formation - Week 13-14: Research and draft development - Week 15: Presentations (May 5-7) - Week 15: Final submissions (May 7)

Possible Policy Topics: - Monetary policy and inflation control - Fiscal stimulus and recession recovery - Long-term growth and productivity - Income inequality and redistribution - International trade and tariff policy - Financial regulation and stability - Climate change and carbon pricing - Healthcare economics and pricing - Education investment and returns - Immigration and labor markets

Format Requirements: - Written brief: Professional Quarto (.qmd) document - Presentation: PDF slides or Quarto HTML presentation - Include references and citations (APA or Chicago style) - All group members must participate in presentation

Grading Criteria for Brief: - Evidence of research (at least 5 credible sources) - Quality of economic analysis (appropriate use of concepts) - Clarity of writing and organization - Originality and depth of thinking - Proper citations and formatting

Assessment Best Practices

Preparing for Success

For Worksheets: - Attend class prepared with assigned readings - Participate actively in discussions - Ask questions when concepts aren‚Äôt clear - Review previous worksheets before exams

For Experiments: - Arrive on time and ready to participate - Follow experiment instructions carefully - Take detailed notes during the experiment - Think critically about results and implications

For Notebook: - Write reflections regularly (don‚Äôt wait until due date) - Make specific connections to course concepts - Use examples from news, your life, or current events - Revise and proofread before submission

For Semester Project: - Start research early; don‚Äôt wait until final week - Meet with your group regularly - Practice presentation multiple times - Peer review each other‚Äôs work before final submission

Important Grading Policies

Late Work

  • Worksheets: 50% credit if submitted within 1 week; no credit after 1 week
  • Notebook Entries: 10% penalty per day late (max 3 days); no credit after 3 days
  • Project Components: Discussed with instructor; typically -10% per day late

Incomplete Grades

  • Granted only in exceptional circumstances
  • Requires written agreement with instructor before end of semester
  • Must be completed within agreed-upon timeframe

Grade Disputes

  • Discuss grading concerns within 1 week of grade posting
  • Come prepared with specific questions or evidence
  • Respectful dialogue and detailed rubrics help resolve disputes

Rounding Policy

  • Final grades are not rounded (92.4% = B+, not A-)
  • Consistent with university policy
